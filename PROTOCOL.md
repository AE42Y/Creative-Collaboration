#### Collaboration protocol

---
**About**

The protocol is assembled from what already exists. What is needed is a structure imposed on existing ones, rigorously.

The record lives in a single shared document — a markdown file. 
The medium doesn't matter. What matters is the structure: 
- Phase-gated, not date-sorted. 
- Each phase has a statement,
- Each statement is agreed to before the next phase opens.

---
#### Phase 0 — Intention. 
Before anything else: a written statement of the real problem. Not the brief as received — the brief interrogated. 
- What is the founder actually trying to achieve?
- Who is the person on the other side of this product or brand?
- What does success look like for them, not for us?
- What assumptions are we making that could be wrong?

This document is agreed to by both parties before any exploration begins. It is the anchor. Everything after it is tested against it.

---
#### Phase 1 — Constraints. 
What is actually fixed? Budget and timeline are obvious, but the more important constraints are the ones no one states: 
- existing assets,
- team capabilities,
- market expectations,
- things the client has already promised stakeholders.

These get named explicitly. The ones that are genuinely fixed are separated from the ones that are assumed fixed but could be questioned. 
This matters because bad design decisions almost always come from solving a constraint that wasn't actually real.

---
#### Phase 2 — Exploration. 
This is where the work happens, but the record has to happen alongside it. 
- Not "here are three directions" — that's the agency presentation model, which hides the reasoning.
- Instead: each direction opened gets a named entry.
  - What was tried.
  - What it revealed.
  - Why it was ruled out or advanced.
The client doesn't receive directions — they receive a map of the direction space and what was learned by moving through it.

---
#### Phase 3 — Decision. 
- What was chosen.
- The explicit reasoning.
- What was left on the table and why.
Both parties sign this. A simple timestamped message — "I agree this is the direction and this is what it means" — is sufficient.
The signature isn't about legal weight. It's about both parties being present for the decision, not just receiving its output.

---
#### Phase 4 — Execution. 
The artifact, linked to the decision that produced it. If anything changes during execution, the change is noted and the reason is recorded. 
Scope drift is one of the main ways the chain breaks — this is where it has to be maintained most carefully.

---
#### Phase 5 — Acceptance. 

Final sign-off with an explicit statement of what was accepted and what it represents. 
- Not "approved" — that's output language.
- This is: this is what we made, this is what it means, this is what we agreed it should do, and I stand behind it.

---
At the end of this, the entire record is exportable. A stranger can open it and understand the chain without explanation. That is the proof of concept.
